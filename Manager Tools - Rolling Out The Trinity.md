Manager Tools started as a management consulting firm. The Management Trinity was rolled out by the Manager Tools team. After they started releasing the Trinity publicly, folks would rush through the model, instead of changing them. This fast rollout led to disaster. Learning a new skill takes time; learning as a group takes longer. Rolling out the Trinity all at once is impossible.

## Overview
1. The Trinity is designed to work together
2. Start with one-on-ones
3. Conduct one-on-ones for 12 weeks
4. After 12 weeks of one-on-ones, announce the feedback model in a staff meeting
5. Give positive feedback to top performers first
6. After three to four weeks, add positive feedback to the rest of your team
7. After six to eight more weeks, add negative feedback for top performers
8. What about a top performer asking for negative feedback
9. Following eight weeks of positive and negative feedback, roll out coaching slowly
10. This rollout plan works in a number of situations
11. It can and does work ala carte, but there are dangers

## The Trinity is designed to work together
The Trinity can be used one part at a time. This is how the Trinity was originally developed. However, each piece brings a new increase in the ability to get things done. Implementing them together will help to exponentially increase productivity.

## Start with one-on-ones
One-on-ones are the most important manager tool that exists; the single most important managerial behavior. Starting to do one-on-ones is the single best use of any managers time. 40% of organizational change is a result of one-on-ones. It may be uncomfortable for a bit; they are so effective that this discomfort will eventually diminish.

This cast doesn't deal with the meetings themselves, but rather the rolling out of them. Step 1: send an email. They recommend pulling the [template](https://www.manager-tools.com/documents/remote-1-1-email) from the Manager Tools website. This will describe what we're going to do and how it's going to work. They recommend that it should sound like it comes from me.

Before I send out the email, I want to figure out which times in my calendar on a recurring basis I'm going to make available for them to choose from. Take the number of directs I have, multiply it by 1.5 - that's the number of timeslots I need to make available in the email. Look out 3-5 weeks out, where the structure of your week is typical. Don't choose time right before or after my boss' staff meeting or their one-on-one with me. Don't offer times before or after my staff meeting. They don't recommend Monday morning or Friday afternoon. I need to be sensitive to my own calendar needs. 

Ask my directs to reply all. Once they are all in, put them in a schedule and send it out to the team. If someone does not reply, send out the schedule and indicate they are still unscheduled. Another option is to just schedule them.

Another thing to consider for the calendar. Getting everyone scheduled is like moving into a new house. You'll move the dishes at least once. So, be ready for changes in the first few weeks. This applies for both me and my direct.

Some folks may still say no. I can use the middle-man test: would I say no to my boss if he asked to start a meeting with me for 30 minutes every week? If I would not say no, then it's reasonable for me to ask.

After we've sent an initial email, they've chosen a time slot, and we've sent them a schedule, we will brief everyone with what, and how, and why we're doing one-on-ones. I should set aside 30 minutes in my next [staff meeting]([https://pocketcasts.com/uploaded-files](https://www.manager-tools.com/2017/01/how-run-your-staff-meeting-part-1-hof)). These topics are a stand-alone series of podcasts, so I should listen to those. The briefing should be based on this; the more transparency, the better. Share the One-on-one note-taking form. Recommend they listen to some casts, like [_Preparing For Your One On One_](https://www.manager-tools.com/2018/01/preparing-your-one-one). They will have questions, you will have questions, so I should make sure to address those.
